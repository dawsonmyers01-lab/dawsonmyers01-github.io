Visual Guide: AI Study Workflow

February 1, 2026

My newsletter is about applied AI for studying and real-world productivity. This week I wanted the site to look noticeably better by adding AI-generated visuals that actually explain the workflow, not just decorate the page.

The main claim

If AI only summarizes, you can feel productive without learning much. What works better is using AI to force retrieval practice (answering questions from memory) and then using AI to build a simple spaced review plan (short sessions spread out over days).

Part 1: Retrieval practice loop (AI as tutor)

The point of this loop is to expose weak spots fast. AI does not just “tell me the answer.” It pushes me to attempt the answer first, then it corrects me and explains why.

Tutor mode prompt

Use this after reading notes or watching a lecture.

Quiz me on [topic]. Ask 10 questions one at a time. After each answer, tell me what I got right or wrong and why. If I do well, increase difficulty.

Weak-spot diagnosis prompt

Use this right after the quiz so the review is targeted.

Based on my answers, list my top 3 weak spots. Give me a 15-minute plan and 6 practice questions focused only on those weak spots.

Part 2: Spaced review plan (AI as planner)

After the tutor loop, I ask AI to schedule review across the next week. I keep it short (10 to 20 minutes) because that is realistic during a busy semester.

Spacing plan prompt

Short, repeatable, and hard to skip.

Create a spaced review plan for the next 7 days (10–20 minutes/day). Tell me exactly what to review each day and include 3 retrieval questions daily.

Iteration evidence (what I refined)

I did not keep the first image drafts. I refined the prompts to make the visuals match the site and stay readable:

  • Removed clutter so the images have more negative space
  • Kept a consistent palette (dark background with tan and teal accents)
  • Made each image “say something” (banner identity, loop explanation, spacing visualization)

Research backbone (why this workflow is not random)

These ideas are supported by learning science research: retrieval practice (the “testing effect”) and spaced practice are among the strongest and most consistently supported study strategies.

  • Dunlosky et al. (2013) — Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques
  • Roediger & Karpicke (2006) — Test-enhanced learning (testing effect)
  • Cepeda et al. (2006) — Distributed practice meta-analysis
